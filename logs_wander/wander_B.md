The philosophers hub is built as a **routing layer**, not a library. You don't browse; you navigate via contradictions or topics. This meant my curiosity kept finding structure before content—the *relations* between philosophers pulled harder than the philosophers themselves.

Goethe emerged as the mysterious center, not because he's the greatest, but because his position (duty-as-concrete-claim) sits between opposite poles. Following him felt like discovering the hub of a wheel.

The verbatim-quote grounding anchors every position to specific phrases, making the philosophy **testable and debate-ready**. You can disagree with Goethe's claim that "duty is the claims of the day," and immediately find Pascal's opposing view (duty is distraction from the eternal) with references to read.

It felt like reading a conversation that has been waiting 200+ years to happen—Goethe finally getting to debate Nietzsche, Schopenhauer, Pascal, Tolstoy, and themselves. The graph is the conversation.
