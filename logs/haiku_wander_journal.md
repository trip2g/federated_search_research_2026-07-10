7. **Confucius, ritual education**: `li`, `ritual-i-forma`, `uchenie` — form (ritual, learning) educates the inner; self-cultivation through structure, not naturalness alone.

### Did I understand the principles?

**Yes, with specific verbatim grounding:**

Example—Epictetus's dichotomy:
- **What I read**: "Of things some are in our power, and others are not: in our power are judgment, will, desire, aversion, and in short whatever is of our own doing; not in our power are body, property, reputation, and office, and in short whatever is not of our own doing."
- **Restatement**: Only your judgments and choices are truly your own. Everything external—your body, possessions, reputation, outcomes—is outside your control. Tranquility comes from accepting this boundary and focusing your will only on what's yours: your opinions about events, not the events themselves.
- **Where it conflicts**: Nietzsche (pathos *requires* striving for externals), Wattles (thought summons externals), Machiavelli (power is exercised over men and circumstances).

Example—Laozi's wu-wei:
- **What I read**: "The sage does nothing, yet nothing remains undone. He does not take and assert will. Things accomplish themselves." The image: water is soft, yet conquers stone.
- **Restatement**: Real power comes not from forcing your will onto the world, but from aligning with the natural flow. Interference creates resistance; non-action allows things to unfold naturally. Water (soft) wears away stone (hard) not by trying harder, but by persistent non-resistance.
- **Where it conflicts**: Confucius (ritual and learning must shape behavior), Nietzsche (excess strength asserts itself), Smiles/Hill (will and effort determine success).
